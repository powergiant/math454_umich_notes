\section{The Fourier transform}

\subsection{Derivation of the the Fourier Transform}

The Fourier Series is a powerful tool for representing functions, but it has a fundamental limitation: it can only represent functions defined on a bounded domain $[-L, L]$. Beyond this domain, when $x$ moves outside of $[-L, L]$, the Fourier series $ A_0 + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left(A_n \cos \frac{n \pi x}{L} + B_n \sin \frac{n \pi x}{L}\right) $ becomes periodic and no longer aligns with the original function $f(x)$.

To solve this problem, we can take limit of $L\rightarrow\infty$ in the definition of the Fourier series. the domain of $f(x)$ coincide with the Fourier series will becomes larger and larger and finally they are the same.

the Fourier series is 
\begin{equation}\label{eq.Fourier_transform_derivation_1}
    f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \alpha_n e^{\frac{i\pi nx}{L}}, \quad x \in [-L, L]
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq.Fourier_transform_derivation_2}
    \alpha_n = \frac{1}{2L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x) e^{-\frac{i\pi nx}{L}} dx
\end{equation}

introducing $\hat{f}_L$ by 
\begin{equation}\label{eq.Fourier_transform_derivation_3}
    \hat{f}_L(\mu) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x) e^{-i\mu x} dx
\end{equation}

then we have 
\begin{equation}\label{eq.Fourier_transform_derivation_4}
    \alpha_n = \frac{\pi}{L}\hat{f}_L \left(\frac{\pi n}{L}\right)
\end{equation}


$L \to \infty$ then $\hat{f}_L(\mu) \to \hat{f}(\mu)$

\[
\begin{aligned}
    f(x) &= \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}_L\left(\frac{\pi n}{L}\right) e^{i \frac{\pi n x}{L}} \cdot \frac{\pi}{L} 
    \approx \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}\left(\frac{\pi n}{L}\right) e^{i \frac{\pi n x}{L}} \cdot \frac{\pi}{L} 
    \approx \sum_{\mu_n=\frac{\pi n}{L},\, n\in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}(\mu_n) e^{i \mu_n x} \cdot \Delta \mu 
    \\ 
    &\overset{L \to \infty}{\approx} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\mu) e^{i \mu x} d\mu.
\end{aligned}
\]
